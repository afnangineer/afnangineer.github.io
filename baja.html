SAE BAJA

drivetrain engineer and finance manager

SAE Baja is a national competitive event that challenges collegiate teams to design and build their own roll cage vehicles to withstand the rough terrain of the course. The competition would make the AWD configuration a requirement of all teams in 2021, and early integration of it would award 2020 teams with additional points per event category. The 2020 drivetrain team decided to design a 4WD system with forward capabilities, allowing future teams more time to focus on testing than reinventing the wheel.

    • DESIGN JUSTIFICATION OF CUSTOM COMPONENTS

    Jackshaft, Chain Drive, and Transfer Case Primary Mounting Plate

    Coupling the CVT directly to the transfer case (TC) would have required modifying its splined input shaft--likely hardened--and offsetting the engine 4" to the left. The CVT secondary and cover would extend below the floor of the chassis, requiring extra members to protect them, and the interface between them would have been difficult to manufacture.
    To circumvent this, a jackshaft and chain drive asssembly would transmit power from the CVT secondary output to the TC input. This introduced a different loading scenario, which was now receiving input from the chain drive rather than the driveshaft.
    Bearing failure on the TC was the first point of concern, as it recessed ~3” from the sprocket. This led the decision to redesign the plate housing the oil seal to include a second bearing, reducing the overhung load to ~1 and increasing bearing life.
    The second point of concern was the challenge with mounting the TC. The existing mounting points lie on a plane that is not parallel to the axis of the input shaft, resulting in a downward pitch (marker for reference).
    With the redesign, The TC was mounted primarily from the plate perpendicular to the input shaft, with a secondary mount bolted to the perimeter of the case.
    Note: Photos are from preliminary subsystem integration phase, hence the tack welds and missing fasteners.

    Transfer Case to Rear Differential Coupler

    The 22 tooth spline profile of the transfer case is male, and the rear differential is female. With Polaris' proprietary spline profiles, reverse engineering the splines would have been necessary to create a custom coupler. The cost-effective and timely solution was to modify an existing Rhino chromoly slip yoke with the 22 male to female coupling capability. The sleeves were turned down to create a coupler of a consistent diameter, and we were able to interface the rear differential and transfer case.

    TESTING AND TUNING THE CVT

    The CVT was identified to be the main source of efficiency loss due to its high variability given the spring preload and belt tension. Testing was performed by collecting RPM of the primary and secondary pulley of the CVT. RPM data was gathered using hall effect crankshaft position sensors mounted to the primary CVT pulley and the jackshaft (CVT secondary output). Changing the springs and fly weights would identify any suboptimal load issues in the drivetrain.
